This research work focuses on a machine learning algorithmic implementation of the hedonic house pricing theory. The theory relates the market value of a differentiated commodity to its composite attributes. Implementation of the theory was done using deep neural networks and ridge regression learning algorithms. Model generalizability improvement was attained using L1 and L2 regularizations. Performance evaluation was based on r-squared and mean squared errors. The outcome of the experiment shows that deep learning has a superior price prediction; however, ridge has the interpretation and inferentiality advantage. The reverberated effect of the housing crisis of the last decade demonstrated that the economies of the United States and most developed nations are intrinsically linked with the housing market. Therefore, a stable housing market is a stable economy. Obviously, an improved housing evaluation mechanism based on large number of house datasets provides a more consistent and reliable evaluation.

Hedonikos, is a Greek word for pleasure. Therefore, hedonics-pricing theory shows the relationship between the price of a differentiated commodity and its utility or pleasure. This implies that consumer pays for the pleasure or utility they derive from the purchase of a commodity through its characteristic features. House, laptops, cars etc. are examples of differentiated commodities. Using the hedonic pricing theory, our experiment demonstrated that a deep learning has a superior predictive capability in estimating the value of a real estate property. It also shows that a ridge regression model have interpretability and inferentiality advantage. While deep learning may be preferred in predicting the price of a differentiated commodity like a real estate property, however, ridge regression is more suitable in explaining the concept of utility based on the set of implicit prices of its characteristic features.

Data Science and Visualization: A Spatio – Temporal Hedonic House Regression Model

This work focuses on an algorithmic investigation of the housing market spanning 11 years using the hedonic pricing theory. An improved pricing model will benefit home buyers and sellers, real estate agents and appraisers, government and mortgage lenders. Hedonic pricing theory is an econometric concept that explains the market value of a differentiated commodity using implicit pricing. Exploiting the spatial dependent nature of the housing market, we created new submarkets. A model was built with the new submarket, while another one was built using the existing submarket. Random forest and LASSO were trained with the two models. We argue that our approach has a considerable impact on the dimensionality of a spatio–temporal hedonic house pricing model without a significant reduction in its performance.

Figure 2. Price vs Time, Box Plot and Trending. Figure 3. Submarket Cluster time series .

Figure 2 shows the price change of houses during the last decade in Howard County for three categories of listings on the MLS. Categories considered include standard sale, short sale and foreclosure sale. In the graph, blue, orange and red thick lines represent minimum, median and maximum close prices respectively. Dash lines represent the polynomial of degree three trending lines. The graph shows that houses listed as foreclosure sales usually sell for lower price. As stated earlier, house characteristic features can be grouped into geographical, social and structural attributes. Since the geographical and social attributes are the same, the foreclosure graph suggests that structural damage has a substantial effect on the values of foreclosed homes. Property listed as short sale have somewhat lower priced but higher than foreclosed properties, suggesting that they are still being maintained either by the seller or the 3rd party lender who holds the mortgage. Standard sale properties sold for highest price, because most homeowners maintain the structural components of their properties.

Figure 3 shows the median prices of houses displayed as a function of time for 10 consecutive years starting in 2007. The 27 cities were grouped into submarkets clusters using k-mean clustering learning algorithm. As shown, 3 clusters were created. This clustering shows a visual picture of where to look for affordable housing if that's the consumers desire or if the preference is for high end properties, it makes it easy for consumer to narrow down their list of cities to target.

 

Figure 4. Geo-Clustered LASSO graph Figure 5. Geo-Un-Clustered LASSO graph

The performance of the cross-validated LASSO is shown in figures 4 and 5. The graphs show the value of the Mean Square Error (MSE) vs the log(lambda) along with the number of explanatory variables. The minimum MSE is shown with the left vertical dashed line while the right vertical dashed line shows one standard error from the minimum MSE.

We have investigated a spatio-temporal hedonic house model using dataset obtained from the MLS, spanning 11 years. Time series of foreclosure sale, short sale and regular sale were analyzed. The study also demonstrated the dimensionality reduction of the dataset for a house hedonic regression using k-means clustering for re-grouping of its submarkets. 27 locational submarkets were re-grouped to 3 using k-means clustering. Geo-clustered and geo-un-clustered models were developed. LASSO learning models show that both models are competitive in terms of performance, however, the geo-clustered has a superior advantage in terms of the numbers of features required for optimization. At the minimum MSE, the geo-clustered model required only 59 characteristic features, while the geo-un-clustered required 80 characteristic features. Also at one standard error, the geo-clustered required only 25 characteristic features while the geo-un-clustered required 38. Furthermore, we have demonstrated using random forest that both models are competitive in terms of accuracy with r-squared values of 0.8019 and 0.8003 for geo-clustered and geo-un-clustered models respectively.
